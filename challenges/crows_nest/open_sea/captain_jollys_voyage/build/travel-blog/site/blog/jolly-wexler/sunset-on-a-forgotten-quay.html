Sunset on a forgotten quay

2026-04-28, 19:42 UTC · by Jolly Wexler

I won't say where I am, exactly. Habit and superstition. But I'll say this much: the lighthouse you can see in the photograph is the only one in this anchorage, it stands on a square stone base maybe ten paces on a side, and the tower above it is painted in five horizontal bands — white, then red, then white, then red, then white at the top — with a domed cap and a little gallery deck under the lantern. It's the kind of light you can pick out from three nautical miles in clear weather, which is exactly what it's there for. There used to be a keeper, then there used to be a relief keeper, and now there's an automated bulb on a timer.

The quay itself was built for the herring fleet that hasn't existed since my grandfather's time. There are still bollards, three of them, spaced like teeth in a tired smile. Hesper is moored to the middle one. The water is so still I can hear the lighthouse's lantern motor turning over — that faint click every twelve seconds that means the gears are still good. I took the tide reading at the top of the hour and posted it to the journal as usual; the cross-reference into the regional weather archive ought to identify the place for anyone who cares to look it up, but I'm not naming it here.

I'll spend the night here. There's a wind shift forecast for the small hours, north-by-northwest, and if it comes in with any teeth I'll need to clear the headland before dawn. If it doesn't, I'll stay another day, walk the cliff path, and try to remember why I started doing this in the first place.

The crew of the Reefborn Five would be appalled at how slowly I move now. So would I, if I had any energy left for appalling.
